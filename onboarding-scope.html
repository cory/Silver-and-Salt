1. Project Objective & Strategic Context

The objective of this project is to build a functional, maintainable, low-code prototype of the member onboarding and payment workflow for the regional membership club operating under the brand name Silver & Salt Capital.

To support compliance under Rule 506(b) of Regulation D and maintain a clear legal separation between membership activity and investment activity, the pipeline enforces data segmentation, presents a required compliance disclaimer before submission, and supports a two-stage manual vetting mechanism: Charge Card → Vet and Meet Applicant → Approve or Refund. The $900 founding-member fee is charged at application; on the rare occasion an applicant is not a mutual fit after the conversation, the payment is fully refunded.

The experience should feel premium, calm, and considered, matching the founding-member price point. Standard membership is $1,000; founding members receive a $100 founding-member discount, so they pay $900. The backend uses Google Workspace tools so the system stays owner-maintainable after handover, with editable copy, pricing, and email text that do not require ongoing engineering support.

Read first: four constraints that shape this build.

1. This needs a backend. The live site is static HTML on GitHub Pages and cannot create Stripe sessions or receive webhooks on its own. A Google Apps Script Web App serves as the backend glue (creating the Stripe PaymentIntent and receiving the payment and refund webhooks). This keeps the system low-code and owner-maintainable.

2. Payment is collected on-domain with the Stripe Payment Element. The payment step uses the embedded Stripe Payment Element (Stripe.js), rather than the off-site Hosted Checkout, so the applicant completes the $900 payment without ever leaving the Silver & Salt Capital site. The element is brand-styled via Stripe's Appearance API (fonts toward Cormorant Garamond and Satoshi where the element allows, lime focus states, cream surfaces, brand border radius), and Apple Pay / Google Pay are offered via the Express Checkout Element. PCI burden stays at SAQ A because card fields remain inside Stripe-hosted iframes. This is a deliberate trade: more build effort (roughly 3 to 5 days) and a payment component that is developer-maintained rather than dashboard-editable, in exchange for full brand continuity at the founding-member price point.

3. Payment is charged at application, then refunded if the applicant is not a fit. The $900 is captured immediately when the applicant pays (a standard automatic-capture charge), so there is no authorization-hold clock to race. After the vetting conversation, the CEO either approves the member or, on the rare non-fit, issues a full refund in Stripe. Refunds can be issued for months, so the decision window is comfortable; aim to complete the call and any refund within 30 days. One cost to note in the brief so it is expected: Stripe keeps its processing fee (about 2.9% + $0.30, roughly $26 on $900) on a refund, so each refunded applicant carries that small non-recoverable cost. This is expected to be infrequent.

4. Legal language requires counsel sign-off. The disclaimer wording, the 506(b) separation, and the referral-credit restraint below are drafted for the contractor to implement, but their legal sufficiency is to be confirmed by Silver & Salt Capital's securities counsel before launch.

2. System Architecture & Data Flow

Platform model. Monarch Capital Collective is the platform that operates one or more membership groups. Silver & Salt Capital is the first group. Each group has its own brand, site, price, copy, and onboarding flow, while sharing one Monarch backend, one member database, one payment system, and one health dashboard. Members only ever see their own group's brand; Monarch stays invisible to them. Build this group-aware from day one: every record carries a group identifier, and per-group settings live in a configuration tab, so launching a second group later means adding a settings row and a branded page rather than rewriting the backend. Only Silver & Salt Capital launches now.

The system is composed of a static brand frontend, a Google Apps Script Web App acting as the backend, a Google Sheet CRM, and Stripe. The flow:

Stage 1: Intake Application (pre-call) • The prospective member submits a short intake form on the brand site, capturing minimal contact details, a referral indicator, and a required compliance disclaimer.

Stage 2: CRM Record Ingestion • The submission is written to the master Google Sheet CRM via the Apps Script Web App, tagged with its group (for launch, Silver & Salt Capital). The record status defaults to Pending Vetting and that group's admin notification email is sent.

Stage 3: Stripe Payment • The applicant completes payment in the embedded Stripe Payment Element on the brand site. The Apps Script backend creates a PaymentIntent for $900 (standard automatic capture) and returns its client secret; the frontend confirms it. The $900 is charged immediately. A webhook on charge.succeeded records the payment and sets the row status to Paid, Pending Vetting. The funds are held by approval, not by Stripe: they are charged now and refunded later only if the applicant is not a fit.

Stage 4: Booking Step • On a confirmed payment, the page advances the applicant (client-side, after Stripe confirms the PaymentIntent) to the booking step on the brand site to schedule the required intake conversation.

Stage 5: Vetting, Approval or Refund • After the one-on-one conversation, the CEO records the decision in the CRM. On a fit, marking the row Approved sends the onboarding invite, appends [Active Member], and gates the investor accreditation questionnaire inside the post-call member setup panel. On the rare non-fit, the CEO issues a full refund in Stripe; a charge.refunded webhook sets the row to Refunded and confirms no funds were retained.

Architecture note for the contractor: the Apps Script Web App must hold the Stripe secret key server-side (never in client JavaScript), expose one endpoint to create a PaymentIntent (returning its client secret), and one endpoint to receive the Stripe webhook (handling both the payment and the refund events). The static frontend only ever calls these endpoints; it never touches Stripe secrets. Every submission carries a group identifier, and the backend reads that group's settings (price, theme, notification email, Stripe product, calendar, disclaimer) from the Groups configuration tab rather than hardcoding any single group's values.

CORS gotcha worth knowing up front: an Apps Script Web App does not return the CORS headers a browser expects, so a normal JSON fetch from the static GitHub Pages site triggers a preflight that fails. The standard workaround is to POST with Content-Type: text/plain (which skips the preflight) and parse the JSON string inside doPost, and to deploy the Web App with access set to "Anyone." Build the frontend calls this way from the start to avoid a day lost to opaque CORS errors. The Stripe webhook does not hit this, since Stripe calls the endpoint server-to-server, not from a browser.

3. Brand UI/UX Standards (Silver & Salt Capital)

The standards below define the Silver & Salt Capital theme, which is group #1. Treat them as a theme the page loads per group (colors, fonts, logo, copy), rather than values baked into the markup, so a future group can supply its own theme on the same page structure.

A. Typography
• Headings and display: Cormorant Garamond (weights 300, 400, 600, 700).
• Body, inputs, labels, buttons, and all UI: Satoshi (weights 400, 500, 700, 900).
• Digits 0 to 9: always render in Cormorant Infant, never Cormorant Garamond. On the live site this is enforced automatically by a unicode-range font-face override. Any display number (prices like $1,000 and $900, step counts, dates) must follow this rule. Do not introduce any other fonts; Inter, Montserrat, Playfair, and Merriweather are not part of the system.

B. Color (60-30-10)
• 60% dominant: cream #FBF8F2 page background, with sand #F4EFE6 for section backgrounds and cards.
• 30% structural: moss #2F3E34 for primary text and dark surfaces, moss-light #4A5E50 for body, sage #7E8E84 for muted labels.
• 10% accent: lime #7CB83F (the Join-flow accent) restricted to active CTAs, progress highlights, and focus states. Gold #C4A47E and rust #D16B4F are available as secondary accents. No navy, no neon, no primary blues.

C. Layout & Whitespace
• Generous vertical spacing between sections (80 to 120px at desktop). Horizontal padding of 48px at desktop, 24px at tablet, 16px at mobile. Content max-width 1100px, with narrower 680px reading columns where helpful.
• Border radius: 4px for buttons and badges, 8px for cards and inputs, 12px for large cards.

D. Interactive Micro-Engagements
• Inline validation: form fields validate format (email shape, character limits) the moment focus leaves the field, surfacing a quiet localized note beneath the field. No full-page reload error states.
• Focus states: active inputs show a subtle border color transition toward lime to give immediate tactile feedback on desktop and mobile.

E. Voice
• Calm, declarative, and approachable. Use the Join page reference: "A few questions, followed by a brief conversation." Always write the full name "Silver & Salt Capital" with the ampersand character. No em dashes or en dashes anywhere in copy. State what something is, rather than what it is not (legal disclaimers excepted).

  • Handle the full client-side lifecycle: a loading state while the intent is created, inline error messages styled to brand, the confirmation result, and advancing to the booking step only on a confirmed payment.
  • Build an Apps Script webhook endpoint that handles two events, locating the matching Sheet row from PaymentIntent metadata in both cases:

Columns map to: Timestamp, Group, Legal Name, Email, Phone, Residential Address, State, Referral Source, Membership Vetting Status (Pending Vetting / Paid, Pending Vetting / Approved / Refunded), Stripe Transaction ID, Refund Policy Acknowledged (timestamp), Investor Qualification Status (Pending Questionnaire / Accredited / Non-Accredited), and System Tags. Guard the trigger so it fires once per row and only on a transition into Approved.
  • Groups configuration tab. Add a Groups tab holding the per-group settings the backend reads at runtime: group name, theme reference (colors, fonts, logo, copy), standard price and any founding discount, Stripe product or price ID, notification email, booking calendar link, disclaimer text, and refund policy text. Residential Address is populated at the post-approval onboarding step (Task 5), not at intake, so this column stays empty until a member is approved and onboarded.

  • Embed a scheduling calendar (Google Calendar appointment slots or a Calendly link) on the booking page so the applicant can lock in the required one-on-one review call. Encourage booking promptly so the conversation, and any refund, happens well inside the 30-day window.

Task 5: Post-Call Accreditation Questionnaire (delayed)

  • Build a post-call destination panel reachable only by applicants the CEO has approved (status Approved, $900 already charged and not refunded). This panel is the primary onboarding hub.
  • Embed a two-part onboarding questionnaire:
    • Part A (Community & profile): full residential address (deferred from intake), chapter choice, educational stream preferences, and event interests.
    • Part B (Regulatory Compliance Gate): self-certification against the accreditation criteria of Rule 501 of Regulation D.
  • Backend routing: if the member self-certifies as accredited and opts into deal information, apply the [Accredited Investor] tag and add their email to the separate investment communication platform. If they select "No," keep them in the insulated [Active Member] community pipeline only.

Task 6: Community Referral Tracking

  • Unique referral codes. Each active member is assigned a unique referral code and shareable link (for example .../join?ref=CODE). The intake form reads the ?ref= parameter and pre-fills the referral field, with the labeled question "Who introduced you to the Silver & Salt Capital community?" and a typed name or email accepted as a fallback. Matching on a code rather than a free-text name avoids the ambiguity that breaks name matching.
  • Credit is earned on approved membership only. Because the $900 is charged at application (before vetting), payment alone does not earn a referral credit. The credit is written only when the referred person is marked Approved and becomes an [Active Member], which is the same approval action described in Task 3 that fires the onboarding invite. Applications, paid-but-not-yet-vetted records, and refunded non-fits earn nothing.
  • Reverse on refund. If an approved member is later refunded (a rare reversal), the charge.refunded webhook voids the matching pending credit so the referrer is not credited for someone who did not stay. Only credits whose referred member remains an active, non-refunded member survive.
  • Reward: a flat $100 membership renewal credit. Each successful referral records a flat $100 credit applied toward the referrer's own next membership renewal. It is account credit toward renewal, never a cash payout, and it never scales with anything the referred member does beyond joining.
  • Guards. Block self-referral (a member cannot refer themselves) and duplicate credits (one credit per successfully converted referral). Support a configurable cap on total credits per referrer for periodic review.
  • Scoped per group. Referral codes and the credit ledger belong to a group: a code maps to one group, and a credit applies to that group's renewals. Record the group on every ledger entry.
  • Ledger. Maintain a dedicated admin tab recording: group, referrer name and code, referred member, conversion date, credit amount, and status (Pending / Applied at renewal). This is the audit trail.

A flat membership credit keeps bookkeeping simple; confirm accounting treatment with your bookkeeper, and confirm the restraint below with securities counsel.

Regulatory compliance restraint: This referral tracking exists only to measure community membership growth. No referral fees, transaction percentages, or finder's bonuses shall be mapped, computed, or paid based on any individual's eventual participation in, or capital allocation to, any private placement or Special Purpose Vehicle operated by the investment entity. This restraint is subject to securities counsel review.

Task 7: Self-Monitoring, Health Checks & Alerting

The pipeline must continuously verify its own health and raise attention the moment any link breaks. This is a first-class requirement, not an afterthought: a silent failure (a dead endpoint, a reset deployment, a webhook that stops arriving) must never go unnoticed.

  • Automated heartbeat. A time-driven Apps Script trigger runs a scheduled end-to-end self-test (at least hourly) that checks each link in the chain: the Web App endpoint responds, a test record can be written to and removed from the CRM, the Stripe API is reachable, the webhook endpoint is live, and the email service is within quota. Each run writes a timestamped pass or fail result to a System Health tab.
  • Health dashboard report. A dedicated System Health tab (and a simple owner-readable view) summarizes status with clear green, amber, and red indicators: time of the last successful applicant submission, time of the last Stripe webhook received, the last heartbeat result per component, and the current count of paid records still awaiting a vetting decision (Paid, Pending Vetting), broken out per group so a failure can be traced to a specific group. Readable at a glance with no technical knowledge.
  • Staleness detection. Because a silent stop is the primary risk, the dashboard flags when no successful heartbeat has occurred within the expected interval, or when no real end-to-end submission has landed within a configurable number of days. Absence of activity is itself treated as a warning state.
  • Alerting that triggers attention. Any failed check or staleness flag sends an immediate alert email (with optional SMS) to tori@silverandsaltcapital.com that names the specific component that failed and its likely cause, so the message is actionable rather than a generic "something broke."
  • Frontend acknowledgment. The intake frontend must read and record the backend's success or failure response for every submission rather than assuming success, so a broken endpoint surfaces immediately. This closes the silent-failure gap where submissions appear to succeed in the browser while nothing is written.
  • Leverage Stripe's native monitoring. Enable Stripe's built-in failed-webhook email alerts, and include a periodic reconciliation check that compares Stripe against the CRM, so every Stripe charge maps to a Paid, Pending Vetting or Approved row and every Stripe refund maps to a Refunded row, catching any drift between the two systems.

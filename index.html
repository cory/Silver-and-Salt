The Opportunity

Two sides of one equation — still unconnected

129,429
women-owned businesses in Utah — up from 31% to 44.5% in four years5

Thousands in the $250K–$5M range. Profitable. Growing. Creating jobs. Too big for friends and family, wrong fit for venture capital. Almost nobody is writing them checks.

~25,000
accredited women investors in the Salt Lake–Park City corridor

Women with the means to invest in early-stage businesses. Most have never been invited to an angel table — not because they're not qualified, but because nobody built the on-ramp.

Connect these two sides and you get more businesses funded, more jobs created, better returns. That's not aspiration. That's math.

The capital system wasn't built against women. It just wasn't built for them yet. Utah's angel ecosystem grew out of SaaS exits through technical founder networks. That's not a conspiracy — it's how ecosystems form. And it means an enormous segment of high-performing founders has been structurally locked out.

Women in Utah are disproportionately building in health, wellness, education, childcare, consumer services, and professional services — the exact categories the youngest, fastest-growing state in the country needs most. These aren't niche businesses. They're responses to structural shortages.

Utah's Paradox

#3 to start a business. #50 for women's equality.

That's not a contradiction. That's a pricing error. Every ingredient for business success exists here — low taxes, fast-growth economy, deep talent, regulatory ease. But the capital infrastructure was built by tech founders investing in other tech founders. The gap between #3 and #50 is where the returns live.
